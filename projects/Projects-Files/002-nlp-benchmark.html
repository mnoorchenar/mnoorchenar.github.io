The Core Idea

From a Surgeon's Words to a Prediction

After every surgery, the surgeon writes a narrative note describing what happened. This project is about one question: what's the smartest way to turn that text into numbers a prediction model can learn from? We tested five strategies — from simple label counts to state-of-the-art clinical language models.

Why Does Text Encoding Matter So Much?

A label encoder sees "laparoscopic cholecystectomy" as an arbitrary integer. A clinical language model understands it as a minimally invasive gallbladder removal with a predictable time profile. The encoding method determines how much medical meaning the model can extract — and our results show the gap is up to 16% in prediction accuracy.

Accuracy Bands

Sentence-BERT achieves the lowest MAE (27.6 min) across all models — outperforming traditional encodings by up to 16%. ClinicalBERT also shows significant gains (p < 0.01).

Contextual embedding gains are largest for complex, variable-length procedures (Whipple, Colectomy) where operative narratives are richest.

Sentence-BERT achieves the highest share of predictions within ±30 minutes, directly enabling more reliable OR scheduling.

So What?

Three Things You Can Take Away

🏥
Text adds real value
Adding unstructured clinical text to structured perioperative variables improved prediction accuracy across all models tested — not just some.
🧠
Sentence context wins
Sentence-BERT — which encodes full sentence meaning — outperforms all other strategies including word-level models like ClinicalBERT.
🔬
Simple encoders fall short
Label encoding, count vectorization, and TF-IDF provided only limited benefit. Hospitals investing in simple text encoding are leaving most accuracy gains on the table.

At a Glance

The question: Which text-encoding strategy best extracts predictive signal from surgical notes? The approach: Benchmark 5 strategies on 180,366 real cases. The answer: Sentence-BERT reduces MAE to 27.6 min and cuts prediction error by up to 16% over structured-only models.
